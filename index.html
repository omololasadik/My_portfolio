Understanding Blood Pressure: What You Need to Know
Health article Â· 2024

Blood pressure is the force of blood pushing against the walls of your arteries. It's measured in millimeters of mercury (mmHg) and recorded with two numbers: systolic (pressure when the heart beats) and diastolic (pressure when the heart rests).

What Do the Numbers Mean?

Normal: Less than 120/80 mmHg

Elevated: 120-129 systolic and less than 80 diastolic

High Blood Pressure (Hypertension): 130/80 mmHg or higher

How to Maintain Healthy Blood Pressure

  • Eat a balanced diet low in sodium
  • Exercise regularly (at least 30 minutes a day)
  • Limit alcohol and avoid smoking
  • Manage stress through relaxation techniques
  • Monitor your blood pressure regularly

High blood pressure often has no symptoms, which is why it's called the "silent killer." Regular checkups are essential for early detection and prevention of serious health issues.

5 Surprising Benefits of Drinking More Water
Health article Â· 2024

Water is essential for life, but most people don't drink enough of it. Staying hydrated does more than quench your thirst â€” it supports nearly every function in your body.

1. Boosts Energy Levels

Even mild dehydration can make you feel tired and sluggish. Drinking enough water helps your body function optimally and keeps your energy up throughout the day.

2. Improves Skin Health

Proper hydration keeps your skin moisturized from the inside out, reducing dryness and promoting a healthy glow.

3. Supports Digestion

Water helps break down food so your body can absorb nutrients. It also prevents constipation by keeping things moving smoothly through your digestive tract.

4. Aids Weight Loss

Drinking water before meals can help you feel fuller, reducing calorie intake. It also boosts metabolism slightly, helping your body burn calories more efficiently.

5. Flushes Out Toxins

Your kidneys rely on water to filter waste from your blood and excrete it through urine. Staying hydrated supports kidney function and reduces the risk of kidney stones.

Aim to drink at least 8 glasses (2 liters) of water daily. Adjust based on activity level, climate, and individual needs.

What is a Job Specification? (And How to Write One)
HR & workplace article Â· 2024

A job specification is a detailed document outlining the qualifications, skills, experience, and attributes required for a specific role. Unlike a job description (which focuses on duties), a job specification focuses on the person who will fill the role.

Why Do We Need Job Specifications?

  • Attracting the right candidates: A clear specification helps job seekers self-select, reducing irrelevant applications.
  • Evaluating candidates: Specifications offer a basis for assessing whether candidates meet requirements.
  • Onboarding new employees: They help new hires understand expectations and responsibilities from day one.
  • Setting performance goals: Clear specifications outline expected outcomes and evaluation criteria.
  • Identifying training needs: They highlight skills gaps and development areas.

How to Write One

Include job details (title, location, type), a job summary, key responsibilities, skills and qualifications, education requirements, work experience, working conditions, and any additional information like performance measurements. A well-written specification should be accurate, specific, clear, concise, free from bias, and up-to-date.

Can My Employee See My Wage Stream? (Here's What You Should Know)
HR & workplace article Â· 2024

The issue of wage transparency depends on company policies, local laws, and cultural norms. In most cases, employees cannot directly access wage information without proper authorization. However, some organisations now embrace more open-pay policies to promote fairness and reduce pay gaps.

What is Wage Transparency?

Wage transparency refers to open communication about salaries and pay within an organisation. The degree of transparency varies: some companies share salary ranges for different positions, while others only disclose individual salaries. Financial privacy remains a fundamental right â€” laws like the Fair Credit Reporting Act regulate access to financial information in the United States.

What Should Employers Consider?

Employee morale: Open-pay policies can increase trust and motivation. When employees understand how their salaries compare, they may feel more valued. However, some may feel underpaid when learning about colleagues' earnings.

Legal considerations: In the US, the National Labour Relations Act protects employees' rights to discuss wages. Some states like Colorado, California, and New York have strict pay transparency laws. Employers must stay informed about local requirements.

Company size and culture: Smaller businesses may find open-pay systems easier to implement. Industries like technology and finance have embraced wage transparency more than others due to competitive pressures.

The bottom line: wage transparency is not a simple yes-or-no decision. Employers must carefully weigh the benefits against the challenges, ensure legal compliance, and consider their workforce's preferences before making changes.
